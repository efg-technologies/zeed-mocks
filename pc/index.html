The Memex We Didn't Get — And the Browser That Might Finally Deliver It

news.ycombinator.com · 287 pts · 42 comments · 3 hours ago

Vannevar Bush described the Memex in 1945 — a personal knowledge system that would augment human memory. Eighty years later, browsers still treat knowledge as a pile of tabs and bookmarks, and AI browsers treat context as a chat history. Neither is the Memex.

Why current AI browsers fall short

The recent wave of AI browsers — Comet, Atlas, Dia — layers a chat interface on top of Chromium without redesigning the underlying primitives. Tabs are still just URLs. Bookmarks are still just metadata. History is a flat list sorted by time, not by relevance or association.

Bolting a chatbot onto Chrome doesn't make it a Memex any more than bolting a calculator onto a typewriter makes it a computer. The problem isn't the UI — it's the data model.

What's missing

Hierarchical context: Profile for role, TabGroup for project, Tab for the immediate object. Each level maintains its own embedding space, its own memory, its own intent model. When you switch tabs, the context switches with you — not because you told the AI, but because it understands the topology.

Extracted intent in a shared memory layer searchable by meaning, not keyword. The browser should remember that you were researching sqlite-vec for the embeddings pipeline two weeks ago, and surface that context when you open a related article today.

"I don't want to chat with my tabs. I want my browser to understand what I'm working on and anticipate what comes next."
— top comment, 127 upvotes

The primitives that matter

A true Memex browser needs three things: a per-tab extraction pipeline that turns page content into structured facts, a vector database that maps those facts to your personal knowledge graph, and an intent model that predicts what you need before you ask.

The question isn't whether AI browsers win — it's which primitives they commit to. Get the data model right, and the interface follows. Get it wrong, and you're just building another chatbot with a URL bar.

Discussion

The thread quickly converges on the observation that most AI browsers optimize for prompt engineering rather than knowledge engineering. Several commenters point to academic work on associative memory systems and semantic trails — exactly what Bush envisioned.

One recurring question: can a browser really hold long-term memory across sessions without becoming a privacy nightmare? The consensus seems to be that local-first, user-owned storage is non-negotiable.
