The Memex We Didn't Get — And the Browser That Might Finally Deliver It

news.ycombinator.com · 287 points · 42 comments

Vannevar Bush described the Memex in 1945 — a personal knowledge system that would augment human memory. Eighty years later, browsers still treat knowledge as a pile of tabs and bookmarks, and AI browsers treat context as a chat history. Neither is the Memex.

Why current AI browsers fall short

The recent wave of AI browsers — Comet, Atlas, Dia — layers a chat interface on top of Chromium without redesigning the underlying primitives. Tabs are still just URLs. Bookmarks are still just metadata. The "memory" lives on the vendor's side, scoped to one account, and evaporates when you switch models.

What's missing, concretely

The fix is hierarchical context. A Profile for role, a TabGroup for project, a Tab for the immediate object. Extracted intent lives in a shared memory layer searchable by meaning rather than keyword.

The question isn't whether AI browsers win — it's which primitives they commit to.

Discussion

Most commenters agree that chat-as-memory is fundamentally broken. Several mention Mem0 and LangMem as promising server-side approaches, but note that browser-native implementations are missing entirely.

"I don't want to chat with my tabs. I want my browser to understand what I'm working on." — top comment, 94 points
