The Memex We Didn't Get — And the Browser That Might Finally Deliver It

Vannevar Bush described the Memex in 1945 — a personal knowledge system that would augment human memory. Eighty years later, browsers still treat knowledge as a pile of tabs and bookmarks, and AI browsers treat context as a chat history. Neither is the Memex.

Why current AI browsers fall short

The recent wave of AI browsers — Comet, Atlas, Dia — layers a chat interface on top of Chromium without redesigning the underlying primitives. Tabs are still just URLs. Bookmarks are still just metadata. The "memory" lives in the vendor's side, scoped to one account, and evaporates when you switch models. A professional who accumulates thousands of decisions a year gets a ChatGPT transcript.

What's missing, concretely

Zeed's bet is that the fix is hierarchical context. A Profile for role, a TabGroup for project, a Tab for the immediate object. Extracted intent lives in a shared memory layer that's searchable by meaning rather than keyword. And the AI is asked to tag its output with "you've seen this before" instead of presenting every answer as fresh.

The question isn't whether AI browsers win — it's which primitives they commit to.
